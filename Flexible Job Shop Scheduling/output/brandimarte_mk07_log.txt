GAME-THEORY MODEL (a microeconomics view of FJSP)
-------------------------------------------------
  Player      : a job - the decision-maker
  Strategy    : its machine choice per operation + its dispatch positions
  Payoff      : U_i = 1/(1 + a*C_i + b*W_i + g*Conf_i + d*Cmax)  (higher = better)
  Game        : all jobs competing for the shared machines
  Solution    : the decoded schedule once every job fixes its strategy
  Equilibrium : a stable schedule where no job can raise its payoff alone
Two jobs interact whenever their operations meet on the same machine: the
earlier one delays the other, so each job re-routes / re-sequences to raise
its OWN payoff, and the schedule settles at an equilibrium.

MODEL
-----
ONE payoff function. Each job is a self-interested player; machines are
shared resources the jobs compete for. Job i's payoff is
    U_i = 1 / ( 1 + a*C_i + b*W_i + g*Conf_i + d*Cmax )
with C_i = completion, W_i = waiting (= C_i - processing), Conf_i =
machine-conflict load (busier chosen machines cost more), and Cmax the
shared makespan that links each job's payoff to global quality. A schedule
is a Nash equilibrium when no job can raise U_i by changing its own
machine assignment or sequence position alone. The reported social
objective is the makespan Cmax.

Search: critical-path best-response with SINGLE-JOB and JOB-vs-JOB moves +
fictitious-play belief learning + Global/Local greedy seeding +
iterated local search + multiple runs.
Run 0 starts from a fully RANDOM profile; later runs are seeded by the
players' learned beliefs and greedy heuristics. Each step makes the best
deviation - a single job re-routing/re-sequencing, or two rival jobs that
share a machine swapping order or rerouting together - until neither any
job nor any rival pair can improve (a pairwise Nash-stable schedule). The
single best run is reported below. A single payoff drives everything.
